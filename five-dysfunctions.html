The Five Dysfunctions of a Team

Patrick Lencioni · Quick Reference Cheat Sheet

The Dysfunction Pyramid — Each Level Undermines the Next

5 · Inattention to Results Ego & status over team outcomes
4 · Avoidance of Accountability Reluctance to call out peers
3 · Lack of Commitment Ambiguity, no buy-in
2 · Fear of Conflict Artificial harmony
1 · Absence of Trust The root cause
⬆ Fix from the bottom up Top = most visible symptom ⬆
Dysfunction 1

Absence of Trust

What it looks like
✗People hide weaknesses and mistakes
✗Reluctance to ask for help or give feedback
✗Jumping to conclusions about others' motives
✗Dread of team meetings — guarded, political
What good looks like
✓Members admit mistakes and weaknesses freely
✓Ask for help without hesitation
✓Focus energy on the work, not office politics
How to build it
→Personal histories exercise — share backgrounds to humanize each other
→Team effectiveness exercise — each person names the single most important contribution of every teammate
→Personality profiles (MBTI, DiSC) — understand different working styles without judgment
→Leader goes first — vulnerability cascades from the top down
Dysfunction 2

Fear of Conflict

What it looks like
✗Boring, "let's all agree" meetings
✗Back-channel politics replace direct debate
✗Personal attacks disguised as debate (or avoided entirely)
✗Critical topics are never put on the table
What good looks like
✓Passionate, unfiltered debate about ideas
✓Issues are resolved quickly — no lingering resentment
✓Meetings are energizing, not soul-draining
How to build it
→Mining for conflict — the leader extracts buried disagreements and forces the team to work through them
→Real-time permission — remind the team mid-meeting "this is productive conflict, keep going"
→Thomas-Kilmann model — understand each person's default conflict style
→Reframe: conflict ≠ personal attack. Disagree with the idea, not the person.
Dysfunction 3

Lack of Commitment

What it looks like
✗Ambiguity about direction and priorities
✗Revisiting decisions in corridor conversations
✗"I'll wait and see" attitude — no real ownership
✗Excessive analysis / "analysis paralysis"
What good looks like
✓Clear direction — everyone knows the priorities
✓Team rallies around a decision even if individuals disagreed
✓Ability to move fast without perfect information
How to build it
→Cascading messaging — after every meeting, agree on what to communicate to your teams and when
→Deadlines — set clear decision dates and honor them. Decide, then act.
→Disagree and commit — consensus is not required; alignment is. Voice concerns, then back the decision fully.
→Low-risk action bias — a good decision now beats a perfect decision too late
Dysfunction 4

Avoidance of Accountability

What it looks like
✗Mediocre performance tolerated to preserve relationships
✗Missed deadlines with no consequences
✗Resentment builds among high performers
✗The burden of accountability falls only on the leader
What good looks like
✓Peers call each other out respectfully — without waiting for the boss to do it
✓High standards are maintained naturally by team pressure
✓People respect each other enough to have hard conversations
How to build it
→Publish goals and standards — make commitments visible to everyone on the team
→Simple, regular progress reviews — brief check-ins create natural accountability moments
→Team rewards — incentivize collective outcomes, not just individual performance
→Accountability between peers is a sign of respect — reframe it as such
Dysfunction 5

Inattention to Results

What it looks like
✗Individual status and ego trump team success
✗Department silos — "my team vs. their team"
✗Stagnation — the team doesn't grow or improve
✗Politics, turf wars, and distraction from outcomes
What good looks like
✓Individuals sacrifice personal credit for collective wins
✓Everyone is energized by shared success — not just their own slice
✓Winning and losing together creates real cohesion
How to build it
→Public scoreboard — make team results visible. What gets measured gets attention.
→Results-based rewards — tie bonuses and recognition to team outcomes, not individual performance alone
→Leader models it — celebrate team wins loudly, downplay personal credit visibly
→Ask regularly: "Is what I'm doing right now contributing to the team's #1 goal?"
Leader Checklist

Questions to Ask Your Team Regularly

Trust
→Do people openly admit mistakes without fear? Does our leader model vulnerability?
Conflict
→Are we having the hard conversations in the room — or in the hallway afterward?
Commitment
→Does everyone leave meetings with clear, unambiguous direction? No one hedging?
Accountability
→Are team members comfortable calling each other out — not just waiting for the leader to?
Results
→Is the team's #1 goal crystal clear, and does daily behavior reflect it?
The Core Insight
★Teamwork is a choice, not a default. Great teams are intentionally built — starting with trust at the base and working up. Skip a layer and the whole pyramid wobbles.
